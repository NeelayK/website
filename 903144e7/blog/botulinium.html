Botulinium toxin is a neurotoxic protein produced by the bacterium Clostridium botulinum and related species. It prevents the release of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine from axon endings at the neuromuscular junction and thus causes flaccid paralysis. Infection with the bacterium causes the disease botulism. The toxin is also used commercially for medical and cosmetic purposes.

There are seven main types of botulinum toxin, named type A–G. New types are occasionally found.[1] Types A and B are capable of causing disease in humans, and are also used commercially and medically. Types C–G are less common; types E and F can cause disease in humans, while the other types cause disease in other animals.[2] Botulinum toxin types A and B are used in medicine to treat various muscle spasms and diseases characterized by overactive muscle. The commercial form is marketed under the brand name Botox, among others. It is also sold commercially as Myobloc and Neurobloc in the European Union.[3] In the United States, Botox and Botox Cosmetic are botulinum toxins produced by the company Allergan. Dysport is a botulinum toxin produced by Ipsen. Chemical Nature

Botulin is incredibly neurotoxic, and is among the most potent toxins known to humankind. Botulin acts at neuromuscular junctions by preventing axon terminals from releasing acetylcholine, an important neurotransmitter. The toxin cleaves SNARE proteins, thus preventing acetylcholine release. This causes flaccid paralysis, and in extreme cases even botulism. Botulin can act at any neuromuscular junction, which means that it can potentially shut down the action of any muscle in its vicinity, even the heart. This makes it a very dangerous and interesting molecule.

However, this very property of Botulin makes it pharmaceutically relevant. Scientists have leveraged this neurotoxicity for applications in treating spasticity (overactive muscle movement), strabismus (improper eye alignment due to muscle action) and most famously in cosmetics, for removing wrinkles by relaxing facial membranes. At the same time, it has also been used for more nefarious purposes. Botulin was famously weaponized in WW2 and remains an active agent in bioterrorism.

Botulin is a protein, and is thus made up of amino acids. It is a 150 kDa protein, and is composed of a heavy chain and a light chain. The heavy chain is responsible for binding to receptors on the cell membrane, while the light chain is responsible for the neurotoxicity. The light chain is a zinc endopeptidase, and cleaves SNARE proteins, which are responsible for vesicle fusion and thus neurotransmitter release.
